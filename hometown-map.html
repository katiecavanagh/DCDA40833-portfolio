My Hometown Map

An interactive Folium map showcasing meaningful locations from my hometown, with personal reflections on each spot.

Reflecting

My basemap design was inspired by my favorite place in Minnesota. My church camp, Cathedral of the Pines, In Lutsen, MN. I grew up going to this camp, met my best friends, grew in my faith, and found who I am. I always looked up to the staff members that worked there all summer and thought they were royalty. I was lucky enough to be one of those staff members this past summer, and again after I graduate, to give back to my church and the place that built me. The image captured tall, dense pine trees in deep forest green surrounding rustic cabins painted a rich, dark red. The overall mood of the photo felt calm, nostalgic, and grounded in nature. I wanted my map to reflect that same feeling. I translated the dark greens of the trees into my land and park color choices, using layered green tones to create depth without overwhelming the map. I incorporated dark red accents inspired by the cabin paint for key design elements so the map would feel personal and intentional. I also kept green as a dominant color throughout the design because it reflects my high school colors, tying another piece of my hometown identity into the map. The goal was to create something that felt cohesive, warm, and connected to memory rather than just functional. One of the biggest challenges was designing the map so it looked good at different zoom levels. When I zoomed out, everything felt really heavy because of the darker greens and reds I was using. It started to look crowded fast. I had to decide what actually mattered at each level and what could fade into the background. I simplified smaller roads and less important features when zoomed out and let more details appear as you zoom in. It took a lot of trial and error to get it to feel balanced instead of overwhelming. On the technical side, I definitely ran into some frustrating moments. Getting my custom Mapbox style to connect properly in Python wasn’t smooth at first, and I had to double-check my tile URL and API formatting multiple times. I also struggled with keeping my colors cohesive between the basemap and my markers. Since I was working with darker tones, it was easy for things to either clash or blend in too much. I found myself constantly adjusting hex codes and re-running the map to see how everything looked together. I used AI tools to help troubleshoot coding errors and clean up parts of my script when things weren’t working. It helped me move past small mistakes faster so I could focus more on making the design feel intentional and personal.
